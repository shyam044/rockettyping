7 Common Typing Mistakes That Are Silently Slowing You Down

📅 March 20, 2026 ⏱ 5 min read ✍️ Rocket Typing Team
Most people who struggle to improve their typing speed aren't making one big mistake — they have several small bad habits that compound. The frustrating part is these habits feel normal because you've been doing them for years. Here are the 7 most common typing mistakes and exactly how to correct each one.

❌ The 7 Mistakes

MISTAKE 01

Using Only 2–4 Fingers

The single most common mistake. Self-taught typists often develop a "hunt and peck plus" style — using 2 index fingers and occasionally a third. This creates an artificial ceiling on speed because two fingers must cover 26 keys. Every key reached by the wrong finger means a longer movement, more hesitation, and more errors.

✅ THE FIX
Learn the standard 10-finger home row layout. Left hand covers A–G, right hand covers H–; . It feels slower for 2–3 weeks, then permanently faster. The investment pays off within a month.
MISTAKE 02

Not Returning to Home Row

Even people who know where home row is often drift away from it after a few words. When your fingers wander from their anchor position, every subsequent keypress requires a calculation — "where am I, and how far is the next key?" This mental overhead slows typing and increases errors, especially on less-common letters.

✅ THE FIX
Develop a conscious "snap back" habit. After every word, briefly confirm your index fingers are on F and J. The raised bumps are there specifically for this. Within a week it becomes automatic.
MISTAKE 03

Prioritising Speed Over Accuracy

Going fast and making lots of errors is a trap. Every backspace costs you two keystrokes — one to delete, one to retype. At 85% accuracy, you are spending roughly 30% of your keystrokes on corrections rather than progress. Your effective WPM is far lower than your raw keypress rate suggests, and you're reinforcing wrong muscle memory in the process.

✅ THE FIX
Drop your speed by 20–30% and focus on 95%+ accuracy for two weeks. Speed will recover and surpass your previous level because you're no longer training errors into your muscle memory.
MISTAKE 04

Resting Wrists on the Desk While Typing

Resting your wrists flat on the desk while actively typing causes two problems. First, it forces your fingers into an awkward angle that slows movement. Second, it puts pressure on the carpal tunnel, which over time causes fatigue and potential repetitive strain injury. Many typists don't realise this is slowing them down because it has always felt normal.

✅ THE FIX
Keep wrists slightly elevated while typing — hovering just above the desk surface. Rest your wrists only when pausing to think, not while actively pressing keys. A wrist rest is for resting between bursts, not during them.
MISTAKE 05

Using the Wrong Thumb for the Space Bar

Many typists always use the same thumb for the space bar, or use it inconsistently. The correct technique is to use whichever thumb is more convenient based on which hand just typed the last letter — alternating naturally. This distributes the load and speeds up the transition between words. Exclusive use of one thumb creates a micro-pause after every word.

✅ THE FIX
Practice alternating thumbs on the space bar. After a right-hand letter, use the left thumb for space. After a left-hand letter, use the right thumb. It takes a few days of conscious effort to make it automatic.
MISTAKE 06

Tensing Up When Making Errors

When a typist makes an error, they often tense their hands and slow down dramatically — or panic-backspace repeatedly. This creates a negative feedback loop: tension causes more errors, which causes more tension. Over time it creates an unconscious fear response around certain letter combinations or words that have caused problems before.

✅ THE FIX
During practice, deliberately relax your hands after each error. Take a breath. Accept that errors are part of the learning process. The goal during deliberate practice is not a perfect score — it is building calm, controlled muscle memory.
MISTAKE 07

Practising Without Measuring Progress

The most underrated mistake: typing without tracking. If you don't measure your WPM, accuracy, and consistency over time, you have no feedback loop. You can't tell if you're improving or plateauing. You don't know which days your technique was better or worse, and you can't identify which specific areas need the most work.

✅ THE FIX
Take a timed typing test every day at the same time and note your WPM, accuracy, and consistency score. Even a simple daily log of three numbers gives you the feedback needed to improve systematically rather than randomly.

Which mistake costs you the most? For most people it is Mistake 01 (wrong fingers) or Mistake 03 (speed over accuracy). Fix those two first — they typically unlock the most immediate improvement — then work through the rest in order.

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know which typing mistakes I'm making?
Take a typing test and pay attention to your accuracy score and consistency score. Low accuracy (under 93%) points to Mistakes 3, 6, or finger placement issues. Low consistency points to Mistake 5 or tension. A slow but accurate result points to Mistakes 1 or 2.
Can bad typing habits be corrected after years of practice?
Yes, absolutely. Muscle memory can be retrained at any age. It takes 2–4 weeks of deliberate practice to overwrite an old habit with a new one. The key is consistency — 15 minutes of focused practice every day beats an hour of casual typing once a week.
Why does my typing get worse when I try to go faster?
Because speed exposes the limits of your current technique. When you push past your comfortable speed, errors multiply, rhythm breaks, and tension increases. This is normal. The solution is to back off speed, fix accuracy, and let speed rebuild naturally from a solid foundation.
