How to Increase Typing Speed: 10 Proven Tips

📅 March 19, 2026 ⏱ 6 min read ✍️ Rocket Typing Team
Whether you're typing 20 WPM or 60 WPM, there's always room to improve. The good news? Typing speed is a learnable skill — not a natural talent. With the right techniques and consistent daily practice, most people can double their speed within a few months. Here are 10 proven tips that actually work.

🎯 The 10 Tips

TIP 01

Master the Home Row Position

Your fingers should always rest on the home row — A, S, D, F for the left hand and J, K, L, ; for the right. This is the foundation of fast typing. When your fingers start from the right position every time, movement becomes efficient and predictable. Every time you finish a keystroke, bring your fingers back to home row. This habit alone can add 15–20 WPM to your speed.

TIP 02

Prioritise Accuracy Over Speed

This is the most counterintuitive but most important rule: slow down to speed up. When you type fast and make errors, your brain never locks in the correct muscle memory — it locks in the corrected version, which is slower. Focus on typing with 95%+ accuracy at a comfortable pace first. Speed naturally follows once your fingers know where to go without thinking.

TIP 03

Stop Looking at the Keyboard

Every time you look down at your keyboard, you lose rhythm and break concentration. Cover your hands with a cloth or use a keyboard skin to force yourself to type blind. It will feel uncomfortable for the first few days, but within a week your fingers will start finding keys automatically. This single habit separates average typists from fast ones.

TIP 04

Use All 10 Fingers Correctly

Many self-taught typists use only 2–4 fingers, which creates a ceiling on their speed. Each finger has designated keys: your left index finger handles F, G, R, T, 4, 5; your right index handles J, H, U, Y, 6, 7. Learning proper finger assignments means every key is reachable with the shortest possible movement. It takes time to unlearn old habits, but the long-term gain is enormous.

TIP 05

Fix Your Posture and Wrist Position

Sit up straight with your feet flat on the floor. Keep your wrists slightly elevated above the keyboard — not resting on the desk. Your elbows should be at roughly 90 degrees. Good posture reduces fatigue, prevents repetitive strain injuries, and lets you type for longer sessions without your hands tiring. Bad posture is one of the hidden limiters of typing speed that most people never address.

TIP 06

Practice with Short, Daily Sessions

Three 15-minute daily sessions beat one 45-minute marathon every time. Short sessions let your brain consolidate muscle memory between practices. Typing is a motor skill — like playing piano or a sport — and motor skills are built through repetition spaced over time, not through exhausting long sessions. Set a daily alarm and treat it like brushing your teeth.

TIP 07

Track Your WPM and Accuracy Consistently

What gets measured gets improved. Take a timed typing test every day — ideally at the same time — and log your results. Seeing your WPM climb from 45 to 55 to 65 over weeks is incredibly motivating. Use Rocket Typing's consistency score to spot when you're rushing and losing accuracy. Tracking also helps you identify which letters and combinations are slowing you down most.

TIP 08

Work on Your Weakest Keys First

Most typists have 4–6 keys they consistently hesitate on. Common trouble spots include Q, Z, X, and number keys. Identify your weak keys during practice and drill them deliberately — type them 50 times in a row, then in context with other letters. Targeted practice on your weakest links delivers faster improvement than general typing practice.

TIP 09

Use Rhythm, Not Bursts

Typing in explosive bursts followed by pauses is slower than typing at a steady, even pace. Think of it like running: a sprinter who runs unevenly will always be beaten by someone with a smooth, consistent stride. Train yourself to type at an even rhythm. The Consistency metric on Rocket Typing measures this directly — aim for 90%+ consistency to build a sustainable fast typing pace.

TIP 10

Type More in Real Life

The best practice is real-world typing. Write longer emails instead of short replies. Take notes by keyboard instead of pen. Chat on your computer instead of phone. Every sentence you type in daily life adds to your muscle memory. When you start thinking in keystrokes — mentally mapping words to finger movements — your speed accelerates rapidly.

Pro Insight: Research on motor learning shows that the biggest gains come in the first 30 days of deliberate practice. If you follow these tips consistently for just one month, you can realistically add 15–25 WPM to your baseline speed.

📊 Realistic Progress Timeline

Here's what you can expect if you practice 15 minutes daily using these tips:

  • Week 1–2: Speed may temporarily drop as you correct bad habits and learn proper finger placement
  • Week 3–4: Speed returns to baseline and starts climbing by 5–10 WPM
  • Month 2–3: Consistent 15–25 WPM improvement as muscle memory solidifies
  • Month 4–6: Diminishing returns set in — each additional 10 WPM takes more work

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to increase typing speed?
With 15–20 minutes of daily practice, most people see noticeable improvement within 2–4 weeks. Going from 40 WPM to 70 WPM typically takes 2–3 months of consistent practice.
What is a good typing speed?
The global average is around 40 WPM. A good professional typing speed is 60–80 WPM. Anything above 80 WPM is advanced, and above 100 WPM is expert level. Most jobs require at least 40–50 WPM.
Is typing speed genetics or practice?
Almost entirely practice. Typing is a motor skill. While some people learn faster than others, virtually anyone can reach 70–80 WPM with consistent deliberate practice. Very few people are at their natural ceiling.
Does keyboard type affect typing speed?
Mechanical keyboards can feel more responsive and reduce fatigue during long sessions, but they do not significantly increase raw WPM. Focus on technique before spending money on hardware.
